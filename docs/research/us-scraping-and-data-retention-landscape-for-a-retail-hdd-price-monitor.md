# US Scraping and Data-Retention Landscape for a Retail HDD Price Monitor

**As of July 3, 2026**, the safest framing for your tool is this: in the United States, scraping **truly public product pages** without logging in, without bypassing technical controls, and without collecting personal data is **materially lower CFAA risk than it was before _Van Buren_ and _hiQ_**. But that does **not** make it “generally legal” in the broad business sense. The main residual exposure for a commercial price-monitor is usually **contract / terms-of-service**, **platform-specific API license limits**, and **copyright or IP issues around storing expressive content and images**, not just CFAA. That is especially true once a site sends a cease-and-desist, imposes bot defenses, requires login, or offers an API with stricter storage terms.

For your use case, the cleanest acquisition and retention posture is usually: **use official APIs where available; store factual fields you independently observe; avoid storing platform-licensed content longer than the license allows; do not scrape logged-in or blocked pages; do not evade anti-bot; and keep a source-by-source retention matrix in code and policy.** That is the posture most likely to survive both legal review and a practical dispute with a retailer or platform.

## Bottom-line risk tiers

For a small U.S. business price monitor, the **lower-risk** pattern is to collect only **publicly viewable price, availability, SKU/model, seller name, and timestamp** from pages that do not require login or session state; use official APIs where offered; honor published API caching rules; identify your user agent; rate-limit conservatively; avoid PII entirely; and stop when a source imposes authenticated access or stronger technical blocking. Post-_Van Buren_ and post-_hiQ_, that posture is much less likely to trigger a viable CFAA theory than scraping behind credentials or bypassing access controls. It still leaves contract and IP issues, but those can be managed much more deliberately.

The **higher-risk** pattern is to scrape **logged-in pages**, create or use **fake accounts**, replay session cookies, defeat CAPTCHA or bot controls, rotate identities to get around IP blocks, keep scraping after a clear cease-and-desist plus technical exclusion, or persist **licensed API content** beyond its allowed cache window. Those fact patterns move you away from the favorable “public data” line in _hiQ_ and toward the “classic CFAA / classic contract breach” line reflected in later cases and DOJ guidance. They also worsen the optics for state-law claims such as breach of contract, trespass to chattels, unfair competition, or IP claims.

A few specific practices belong in the **red zone** for your project design: storing Amazon Product Advertising / Creators content beyond 24 hours; storing Amazon images rather than image links; using eBay public-site scraping rather than eBay’s developer stack where the public User Agreement forbids robots / scrapers absent permission; treating Google search results or snippets as a persistable dataset; and copying retailer product images into your own long-term image store without a license.

## Current CFAA landscape for public scraping

The Supreme Court’s June 3, 2021 decision in **_Van Buren v. United States_** narrowed CFAA liability for “**exceeds authorized access**.” The Court held that the statute reaches someone who accesses areas of a computer system that are off-limits to them, not someone who merely misuses information they were otherwise allowed to access. In other words, the CFAA is now read more like a **gates-up-or-down** access statute than a general “computer misuse” rule.

The Ninth Circuit’s April 18, 2022 post-remand opinion in **_hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn_** applied that narrower approach to public scraping. The court reaffirmed that _Van Buren_ **reinforced** its earlier view that CFAA “without authorization” is at least seriously questionable when the target material is **publicly available** and the site generally does not require prior authorization to access it. The panel emphasized that the dispute concerned **public LinkedIn profiles viewable by anyone with a browser**, and it rejected the theory that a cease-and-desist alone necessarily converts access to a public website into CFAA-prohibited access. That is the strongest modern appellate authority favoring public-web scraping in the United States.

But the favorable reading is **not unlimited**. The same Ninth Circuit in **_United States v. Sullivan_** in March 2025 described use of **stolen credentials** to access **private servers** as a “typical CFAA violation,” and it quoted _hiQ_ for the opposite proposition: the violation line is crossed when someone circumvents generally applicable access permissions such as username and password requirements. That means your architecture should treat the presence of a login wall, session-only data, or credentialed access as a hard boundary, not something to work around.

The Department of Justice’s current CFAA charging policy, updated in May 2022 and still posted in 2026, follows the same direction. DOJ says it will **not** bring “exceeds authorized access” cases based solely on **contracts, terms of service, or employee policies**, and it says a CFAA prosecution may not be based merely on violating an Internet service’s terms of service for public websites. But DOJ also says that, once authorization is **expressly revoked** through unambiguous written communications that the defendant receives and understands, DOJ may treat further access as unauthorized. DOJ’s policy is not binding on private plaintiffs or civil courts, but it is a strong signal about current federal criminal enforcement posture.

The law is still **unsettled outside the clean public-data fact pattern**. Recent district-court decisions in the Northern District of California against Bright Data have generally continued the trend that **logged-off scraping of public pages** is harder to fit into CFAA or related access claims, but those are not Supreme Court rulings and not all are appellate precedent. In January 2024, the court in **_Meta Platforms v. Bright Data_** granted summary judgment for Bright Data on claims tied to logged-off scraping of public Meta pages, and in May 2024 the court dismissed X’s complaint against Bright Data after finding X had not plausibly alleged the relevant access-based theories. These are useful signals, but they do not erase source-specific contract and IP risk, and they should not be read as a universal permission slip.

The cleanest practical reading for counsel is therefore: **relatively settled** that CFAA risk is much lower for scraping pages open to anyone without login or technical bypass; **still risky and fact-sensitive** once the source imposes authentication, individualized revocation plus technical exclusion, or meaningful access barriers; and **not settled at all** as to how far a source can go with parallel contract, tort, or IP claims even when CFAA fails.

## Contract and terms-of-service risk is different from CFAA risk

Even when CFAA is weak, **breach-of-contract claims can still be strong**. That is the key lesson many teams miss after reading _hiQ_. CFAA is a federal anti-hacking statute. A ToS claim is usually a **state-law contract claim** asking whether the scraper assented to the terms and then violated them. A scraper can win on CFAA and still lose on contract. That is substantially what happened in the longer _hiQ_ saga: after the appellate CFAA win, LinkedIn later obtained summary judgment on its contract theory tied to hiQ’s use of **fake / logged-in accounts**, and the case ended with a consent judgment and injunction. The important nuance is that the final contract result was driven by the narrower, worse facts, not a final merits ruling that all public scraping is contractually forbidden.

For enforceability, **clickwrap and clear sign-in-wrap** are usually much easier to enforce than passive browsewrap. In **_Nguyen v. Barnes & Noble_** the Ninth Circuit held there was no evidence the user had actual knowledge of the agreement where terms were just hyperlinked in the footer. In **_Berman v. Freedom Financial Network_** the Ninth Circuit again refused enforcement where the terms notice was inconspicuous and users did not unambiguously manifest assent. By contrast, in **_Meyer v. Uber_** the Second Circuit found enforceable assent where the app used a clear prompt telling users that by creating an account they agreed to the hyperlinked terms, and the layout made the notice reasonably conspicuous.

For scrapers, one older but still important case is **_Register.com v. Verio_**. There, the Second Circuit treated repeated automated access after notice of use restrictions as enough to support contract liability even though the defendant did not click an “I agree” button each time. That case matters because a commercial scraper that repeatedly queries a site with actual notice of restrictions can look less like a casual browser and more like a party knowingly taking a benefit on offered terms.

For your project, that means **browsewrap vs. clickwrap does matter, but not in a comforting way**. If a retailer’s anti-scraping language is buried and you never create an account, contract formation may be weaker. If your workflow requires an account, sign-in, API key, developer enrollment, or repeated use after specific notice, contract formation gets much easier for the site to prove. And some sites now expressly prohibit scraping in currently effective public terms. eBay’s User Agreement, effective June 28, 2026 for existing users, says users will not “use any robot, spider, scraper, data mining tools, data gathering and extraction tools, or other automated means” to access eBay services without prior express permission. That is not a CFAA rule; it is a contract / platform enforcement rule.

The practical consequence is simple: if you rely on a source’s public website rather than its official API, treat the question as **“what claims could they plausibly bring even if CFAA is weak?”** not just “is this public?” Public visibility lowers one category of risk. It does not eliminate the platform’s contractual remedies, account shutdown rights, or leverage in a cease-and-desist.

## Copyright, database rights, and what you can safely keep

Under U.S. copyright law, **facts are not protected**. The Copyright Office says copyright does not protect “facts, ideas, systems, or methods of operation,” and the Supreme Court in **_Feist_** held that the white-pages facts in a telephone directory were not copyrightable. For your tool, that means **observed prices, availability states, seller names, model numbers, timestamps, and your own derived price-history series** are on the safest footing. A database of factual observations you independently collect over time is substantially safer than a copied corpus of expressive product content.

Where teams get into trouble is by sliding from **facts** into **expression**. Product names, bare specs, and short snippets may be too short or too unoriginal to attract copyright protection in many instances; the Copyright Office specifically says names, titles, and short phrases are not protected. But longer **product descriptions**, editorial copy, buying guides, comparison charts, and many review snippets are much more likely to contain enough originality to be protected. That is an inference from the Copyright Office’s basic originality rule and its treatment of short phrases. In practice, that means storing only the **minimum necessary text** is materially safer than warehousing full retailer descriptions.

**Images are the highest-risk content type** in your proposed dataset. The Copyright Office explains that photographs, illustrations, and other pictorial works are copyrightable original works. So if your monitor stores retailer or manufacturer images in your own database or object store, that is usually much harder to defend than storing factual price records or even short product titles. Unless a source license expressly permits image storage, the conservative policy is to store only **image URLs, hashes, or your own normalized metadata**, not copies of the images themselves.

On **database rights**, the United States does **not** currently have an EU-style sui generis database right that protects non-original databases merely because substantial money was spent compiling them. The U.S. Copyright Office’s database reports discuss the EU Database Directive and multiple proposed U.S. database bills, which underscores that this kind of right was **considered rather than enacted** in the United States. WIPO’s database materials likewise distinguish copyright protection for original selection/arrangement from separate sui generis protection deployed in the EU. For a U.S.-focused monitor hitting U.S. retail sites, that means there is no standalone American “database right” equivalent to the EU regime. If you later expand into EU retailers or materially target the EU, that issue should be revisited with counsel.

## Robots.txt and practical compliance hygiene

As a matter of protocol, **robots.txt is real**: RFC 9309 made the Robots Exclusion Protocol an IETF standards-track document in September 2022. But the RFC is explicit that robots rules are **“not a form of access authorization.”** Legally, that means robots.txt is not itself the same thing as a password gate or API authorization token. Ignoring it does not automatically create CFAA liability the way bypassing credentials can.

Practically, though, robots.txt still matters a lot. First, it is evidence that a site signaled its preferences and boundaries. Second, some contracts incorporate or parallel those restrictions. Google’s current Terms of Service, updated May 22, 2024, forbid “using automated means to access content” from Google services **in violation of machine-readable instructions** on Google web pages, including robots.txt. So while robots.txt is not itself access authorization, violating it can become evidence of **contract breach** or bad faith.

For a good-faith business monitor, the most defensible operational hygiene is therefore to: use a stable and truthful user-agent; publish a contact page or abuse mailbox; honor robots.txt absent a deliberate counsel-approved exception; obey rate limits and backoff; avoid CAPTCHA solving or stealth / evasion tooling; stop on authenticated flows; avoid scraping any user reviews, customer Q&A, or seller profile data that could contain personal data; and maintain an internal source registry that records each source’s ToS / API basis, robots posture, refresh cadence, and allowed retention. That playbook lines up with both the legal authorities above and the direction of current official platform terms.

## Recommended acquisition and retention policy

A short policy that should be defensible in front of counsel would look like this.

**Acquisition hierarchy.** Use an official API whenever one exists and is commercially workable. If no official API exists, scrape only **public, unauthenticated product pages**. Do not create accounts solely to access product pages. Do not use fake accounts, saved sessions, CAPTCHA solving, stealth plugins, rotating residential proxies, or any other anti-bot evasion to obtain data. Stop collection from a source once the source moves the relevant data behind login or imposes stronger technical blocking.

**Data minimization.** Persist by default only the fields needed for price-history analysis: source, canonical product URL, normalized merchant SKU / MPN / UPC / ASIN / ePID where applicable, observed title, observed price, currency, shipping flag if clearly displayed as a fact, availability / stock state, seller / merchant name, scrape timestamp, and confidence / normalization metadata. Treat full descriptions, reviews, editorial text, and images as **non-default fields** that require source-by-source legal approval.

**Content retention.** Keep **factual observations** such as price and availability history for your normal business retention period, since those are your own observed facts. For **licensed API content**, enforce the strictest source term in code. Today that means, at minimum: Amazon Product Advertising / Creators content other than ASINs should be TTL’d at **24 hours**; Amazon images should not be stored, only image links for up to **24 hours**; eBay displayed listing data should be refreshed such that displayed item listings are no more than **6 hours** stale; Google Programmable Search results should be treated as **transient only**; Serper / Google-derived result snippets should be treated as **discovery metadata**, not durable content.

**Robots and source controls.** Honor robots.txt by default; use a truthful user-agent; publish contact info; apply conservative rate limits and exponential backoff; respect caching headers where feasible; and keep an auditable per-source config showing robots status, public ToS position, API authority, refresh interval, retention TTL, and last legal review date. Since robots.txt is not authorization in itself but is often incorporated into broader platform restrictions, your default should be to honor it unless counsel approves a specific exception.

**PII and user content.** Do not collect reviews, customer Q&A, seller profile details, usernames, or anything that could plausibly be personal data unless a separate, approved use case requires it. That is both legally cleaner and operationally easier. eBay’s agreements in particular impose deletion and privacy duties around user information, and DOJ’s CFAA policy expressly aligns federal enforcement with protecting confidentiality and integrity of systems and data.

**Escalation triggers.** Trigger legal review before continuing collection if any source sends a cease-and-desist, blocks your user-agent or IP range, changes ToS to expressly prohibit your workflow, introduces login-only access, or offers a paid data feed or developer program that is a realistic substitute. Those are the moments where “good-faith public monitoring” stops looking like a neutral public-facts collector and starts looking like a party deliberately overriding a source’s chosen access channel.

**eBay clause scoping (clarification).** The pricing-tool **written-consent** bar is scoped to **"Restricted APIs Data"**; the **Browse API is not a Restricted API** (those are trend/pricing products, e.g. Marketplace Insights), so Browse-only use does not trip it. The applicable limit on a home-grown price feed is the separate **§8 bar on using eBay Content to "suggest or model prices for items listed on eBay Site"** — storing observations to *alert* is low-risk; computing a modeled *fair price* for eBay items is the gray area **[LAWYER]**. The **seller-arbitrage/auto-repricing** bar (§6) does not apply to a buyer's monitor. **No personal / non-commercial exemption exists** — the license binds all Developers identically (and this project is business-purposed regardless).

**Defensible-scraping guardrails (encode as acquisition policy).** Public **logged-out pages only**; **never** log in / create accounts / bypass anti-bot / CAPTCHA / IP blocks; **`ROBOTSTXT_OBEY=True`**; **AUTOTHROTTLE** + honor `429`/`Retry-After` (no server degradation → no trespass-to-chattels harm); honest User-Agent; **prefer official APIs**; **store facts not expression**; **no PII**; low-volume; **stop on a specific cease-and-desist**. This keeps the tool inside the CFAA-safe zone (_Van Buren_ / _hiQ_ / _Meta v. Bright Data_, which stands — no appeal) and starves the surviving state-law contract/trespass theories (_Reddit v. Anthropic_, remanded to state court 2026) of their elements.

**Corrections to this report's original claims** (re-verified 2026-07-03): SP-API "30→7 day" retention shift — **removed** (current: 30-day PII / 18-month non-PII); eBay anti-automation clause — clarified (AI/LLM scraper ban first effective **2026-02-20**; the "June 28, 2026" date is the current User Agreement version); Brave — added (free tier removed Feb 2026, standard ≈$5 CPM, storage-rights plan required to persist).

## Open questions and limits

A few issues remain genuinely unsettled and are worth flagging for counsel rather than answering too confidently here. The first is **how far a cease-and-desist plus partial technical blocking goes outside the Ninth Circuit** for scraping otherwise public pages; _hiQ_ is favorable but fact-specific and not the final word everywhere. The second is whether recent district-court scraping wins for Bright Data will hold up if materially different facts reach appellate courts. The third is the exact **public-retail use fit of Amazon SP-API**, because the public materials I found focus on restricted seller / customer data rather than a clean public-market-data retention rule for your use case. And the fourth is **Google-derived SERP APIs**: Serper’s own terms are permissive, but the rights chain and Google’s own restrictions remain a live litigation and platform-risk issue.

The safest counsel-ready thesis, therefore, is not “public scraping is legal,” but rather: **public factual price collection is materially lower CFAA risk after _Van Buren_ and _hiQ_, while the decisive compliance work for this product is source-by-source contract and API-term governance, aggressive avoidance of technical circumvention, and strict minimization of expressive content retention.**
