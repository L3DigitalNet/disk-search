# OEM Part-Number Cross-Referencing for Server-Pull HDD/SSD Listings (Dell/EMC, HPE, Lenovo/IBM, NetApp)

## Bottom line

OEM part numbers are **not a reliable 1:1 key to a manufacturer MPN** for any of the four vendor families studied (Dell/EMC, HPE, Lenovo/IBM, NetApp) — the relationship is **many-to-many**, and the clearest evidence is a NetApp part number, `X477A-R6`, that resellers list as equivalent to **both** an HGST-branded model (`HUS724040ALS640`) **and** a Western Digital-branded model (`WD4001FYYG`) — the same OEM-qualified spec sourced from two different manufacturers over the part's production life. HPE's own hard-drive documentation states the same thing from the other direction: "a hard drive model can be used in multiple products with one assembly part number, but the spare and option parts may differ." This confirms the concern already flagged in [ADR 0018](../adr/adr-0018-manufacturer-spec-catalog.md): an OEM part number can resolve reliably to `product_family`/`product_model` grain, but **must not** be trusted to resolve to an exact `product_variant` (firmware/qualification-specific) without corroborating evidence.

No OEM vendor publishes a public, bulk, machine-readable OEM→MPN table. **Dell and NetApp have no public catalog at all** — Dell's own support staff direct customers to reseller/premier-account channels, and NetApp's Hardware Universe requires a customer or partner login. **HPE PartSurfer is the sole public, free, per-part lookup tool** among the four, but it has no official API (only a browser-facing web app; third parties scrape it) and returns single-part results, not a bulk export. **Lenovo/IBM's official tools locate FRUs by machine serial/type, not by a manufacturer-MPN cross-reference field.** Given this, the highest-leverage population strategy for a single-maintainer tool is **not** pre-seeding from an official bulk source — none exists — but **lazily learning the alias table from refurbisher and marketplace listings that self-label both numbers**, backed by a small manual backfill queue for gaps (the queue ADR-0018 already names). This is corroborated by direct evidence: third-party refurbisher product pages (EMC SPS Pros, ServerSupply, Alta Technologies) and even eBay listing titles routinely print the OEM PN, the OEM's own internal alias numbers, *and* the underlying manufacturer model number together on one page — exactly the dual-labeled training data the strategy needs.

## OEM part-number semantics by vendor

### Dell / Dell EMC

Dell operates **two distinct numbering systems** that this project's listings will encounter, and they are frequently confused with each other:

- **Dell Precision/PowerEdge component part numbers (DP/N)** are a Dell-wide part-numbering scheme stamped on a small "DP/N 0XXXXX" label on the physical part. The leading `0` is a filler digit; the following 5 characters are the Dell part number proper. This is Dell's **spare/replacement-part identifier**, used for parts across servers, desktops, and laptops alike, not a drive-specific scheme.
- **Dell EMC storage-array part numbers (TLA — Top Level Assembly)** are a *separate*, older EMC numbering convention still seen on CLARiiON/VNX/PowerStore-era drive carriers. It is genuinely decodable: a 9-digit number `PN 005xxxxxx`, where the first 2 characters after the family code indicate the **array family** (`CX`, `AX`, `VX`/`V2`/`VS`/`V3`/`V4` for VNX), the next 4 characters encode **drive interface + speed** (e.g. `2G10` = 2 Gb/s Fibre Channel at 10K RPM, `SA07` = SATA 7.2K, `VS15` = VNX SAS 15K), and the trailing 3 digits encode a **capacity bucket** (e.g. `010` = 1 TB, `030` = 3 TB). This decode ladder is documented informally by third-party support blogs, not by Dell/EMC itself.

**1:1 vs 1:N verdict — confirmed N:1 (and drifting).** Dell/EMC's own documentation and community evidence show that **multiple part numbers map to the same functional item over time**:
- The TLA scheme explicitly allows this: "There are several TLA part numbers that fall under the same EMC model number" — EMC's own support guidance says a drive with TLA `005048797` and its replacement `005049070` are "both the same EMC hard drive model number ... and are hot-swappable," i.e., the model number is the more stable grain and TLA changes underneath it (a running-change/qualification-batch artifact).
- A widely-cited PowerStore firmware-release note lists **eight distinct part numbers** (`005052919`–`005052926`) all taking the exact same firmware image (`VPV1ET13`), and repeats the pattern for three more firmware versions — direct vendor evidence that one physical/firmware-equivalent drive corresponds to many Dell EMC part numbers simultaneously.
- On the DP/N side, a well-documented r/Dell case shows four part numbers (`G176J`, `8FKXC`, `NTPP3`, `NRX7Y`) that Google Image Search shows as visually identical, which the poster later resolved to a genuine (if minor) hardware difference — plastic vs. metal internal rails between the pairs — illustrating that **DP/N differences can encode real, if subtle, physical revisions**, not just cosmetic SKU churn; you cannot assume "different DP/N ⇒ different drive" or "different DP/N ⇒ same drive" without a case-by-case check.

**Access posture (2026).** Dell has **no public parts catalog for PowerEdge/storage components**. Multiple current Dell Community moderator/staff replies (2026) confirm this directly: "I am not aware of any part list we publish to the public... get in touch with your sales team... reseller or Dell premier account privileges." Dell's own community members note the asymmetry with HPE explicitly: "HP is a piece of cake — simply type the server model number in Google followed by 'quick specs' ... HP cant be the only ones who make their parts easy to find?" The consumer-facing `dell.com/en-us/shop/mpn-sitemap` and `dell.com/en-us/shop/partsforyourdell` tools are service-tag/retail-product lookups, not a drive-specific manufacturer-MPN cross-reference, and do not cover enterprise/server drive parts in bulk.

### HPE (and legacy HP/Compaq)

HPE is the **best-documented and only publicly accessible** of the four systems, and it is explicit that a single component has **three separate, non-interchangeable identifiers**:

1. **Option part number** (e.g. `-B21` suffix) — the SKU used to *order* the part new or as part of a factory configuration.
2. **Spare part number** (e.g. `-001` suffix) — the SKU used to order a *replacement* under warranty/support (self-repair or on-site service).
3. **Assembly part number** (a shorter numeric/random-suffix code, e.g. `-071`) — the manufacturing-build identifier; this is what iLO/management firmware actually reports for an installed component, which is why administrators trying to cross-shop a part often start from the *wrong* number.

A worked example from HPE Community: **Option `507125-B21` / Spare `507283-001` / Assembly `518011-001` / HPE Model `EG0146FARTR` / GPN `507129-001`** — five different codes for what is represented as one component, only one of which (`EG0146FARTR`) resembles a manufacturer-style model string, and even that is an HPE-assigned model designation, not necessarily the underlying Seagate/WD/Toshiba MPN verbatim.

**1:1 vs 1:N verdict — confirmed many-to-many, stated by HPE itself.** The official HPE "Hard Drive Model Number Matrix" document states plainly: *"A hard drive model can be used in multiple products with one assembly part number, but the spare and option parts may differ."* Community threads corroborate the reverse direction too — for a pair of 128 GB DIMMs, "the Option part number is different... the Assembly part number is almost identical but it is `-1A1` vs `-0A1`... The spare part number however is the same," and an HPE-internal system confirmed two assembly numbers were functionally identical even though PartSurfer's public UI did not know it. So: one HPE spare number can span multiple assembly/option numbers, and one physical manufacturer part can be sold under **multiple** HPE option/spare identities depending on target platform and channel. Cross-reference must therefore be treated as **fan-out in both directions**, matching the ADR-0018 concern precisely.

**Access posture (2026).** `partsurfer.hpe.com` is HPE's official, **free, public** lookup tool — no account required, confirmed by HPE's own FAQ ("We are happy to provide PartSurfer at no cost"). It performs bidirectional lookup (enter option, spare, assembly, or model and see the others) and links to system/option-kit compatibility, but it has **no official public API**; the two GitHub projects found (`georgeglessner/HPEPartSurfer`, forked by `dhayner`) are **unofficial** scrapers, and a Reddit commenter in the same thread warns "scraping the HTML is not a good long-term API especially on HPE's notoriously terrible website. Be prepared to continually update it." A commercial product (`itadtools.com`) advertises exactly this fragility as its value proposition — it "reads HPE's data feeds directly and keeps its own drives database and cache" via "API calls and browser automation (Playwright) for cases where the API doesn't return complete data," explicitly because `partsurfer.hp.com` (the legacy HP Inc. consumer site, distinct from the enterprise `partsurfer.hpe.com`) has been reported down repeatedly through 2026. HPE's Terms of Use ("By using this site, you accept the Terms of Use and Rules of Participation") were not found to carry an explicit "no scraping" clause in what is publicly indexed, but the pattern of third parties building resilience layers around it, rather than using a sanctioned API, suggests treating it as **interactive/manual lookup only** — fine for a backfill queue processed at low, human-paced volume, risky as an automated bulk-import source.

### Lenovo / IBM

Lenovo (successor to IBM's x86 server business) and IBM (mainframe/legacy Power/System x, and the FRU terminology's origin) use a **marketing part number vs. FRU (Field Replacement Unit) part number** split, which is conceptually close to HPE's option-vs-spare split but has its own wrinkle:

- **Marketing part number** — used to originally **purchase** a part (new-build or upgrade), analogous to HPE's option number.
- **FRU part number** — used when a **technician or self-maintainer replaces a failed part** under warranty/service; IBM's own support KB frames the choice explicitly around "are you buying it, or are you claiming warranty repair" as the decision key.
- **"MultiFRU"** — an IBM designation meaning the *same marketing part number* has **different, region-specific FRU numbers** depending on the country of purchase or service. This is a documented, official instance of **1:N by design**, not drift: one marketing part number legitimately fans out to several FRU numbers split along geography.
- A ThinkPad-forum case shows the FRU itself can also fan out by a genuinely different attribute — leaded vs. RoHS/unleaded builds of the same functional part (`39T0361` unleaded vs `27K9976` leaded for the same panel) — another axis of legitimate one-part-many-FRU fan-out that has nothing to do with the underlying manufacturer changing.
- Lenovo/IBM's own KB notes an **operational failure mode** relevant to entity resolution at ingest time: newer Lenovo-branded System x hard drives were found to **not report FRU information at all** to management tools (DSA, StorCLI) — "the FRU field will either be blank or missing" — meaning a nontrivial fraction of Lenovo-pull drives will carry **no OEM part number signal whatsoever** in machine-readable inventory data, only on a physical label (drives are labeled `LENOVO-X`, older ones `IBM-X`).

**1:1 vs 1:N verdict — 1:N by explicit design (MultiFRU) plus incidental fan-out (build revisions), and a real "1:0" data-quality risk** (FRU absent from reported inventory). No evidence was found of IBM/Lenovo publishing a manufacturer-MPN cross-reference for drives; what exists is the opposite kind of cross-reference.

**Access posture (2026).** IBM's own KB page titled "Hard disk drive part number cross-reference (US and EMEA) - Servers" is **not** an OEM-to-manufacturer-MPN table — its one-line description is explicit: **"Cross-reference between EMEA and US hard drive part numbers"** — i.e., it reconciles IBM's own two regional part-numbering schemes against each other, not IBM parts against Seagate/WD/Toshiba MPNs. Lenovo's official tool, `support.lenovo.com/us/en/parts-lookup` (aliased `support.lenovo.com/partslookup`), is a **serial-number/machine-type-driven** parts lookup for ordering — enter a product serial or browse a machine type to see compatible FRUs — with **no bulk export and no manufacturer-MPN field** surfaced in the public UI. A legacy precedent exists (an archived, community-hosted 1998 "IBM PC300GL FRU-P/N Cross-Reference List" PDF found via `sharktastica.co.uk`) showing IBM did once publish system-specific FRU/marketing-PN cross-reference documents for individual product lines, but nothing equivalent was found live for current server drives, and even that legacy document doesn't reach down to the underlying drive manufacturer's MPN.

### NetApp

NetApp's disk part numbers follow an **`X` + digits + optional revision suffix** convention (e.g. `X477A-R6`), colloquially the "X-article" scheme. NetApp additionally assigns **its own internal numeric alias** in parallel (e.g. `108-00315`) and a distinct "SP-" spare-part alias (e.g. `SP-477A-R6`) for what resellers treat as the same orderable drive — so NetApp alone already carries **at least three parallel identifiers** for one part before you even reach the manufacturer's model number.

**1:1 vs 1:N verdict — confirmed N:N, with the strongest evidence found in this research.** Reseller and marketplace listings for `X477A-R6` (a 4 TB 7.2K NL-SAS drive for the DS4246/FAS2240-4/FAS2554/FAS2520 shelf family) consistently cross-reference it to **`108-00315`**, **`SP-477A-R6`**, and the manufacturer model **`HUS724040ALS640`** (an HGST/Hitachi Ultrastar 7K4000) — but a separate, contemporaneous eBay listing for the *same* `X477A-R6` part number instead cross-references it to **`WD4001FYYG`** (a Western Digital-branded model). Both listings are for drives sold under the identical NetApp part number and identical rated spec (4 TB, 7.2K, SAS). This is direct, vendor-agnostic-marketplace evidence that **one OEM part number was fulfilled by physically different manufacturer drives (different brand, likely different firmware/qualification) across its production run** — the clearest confirmation in this research that OEM PN resolution belongs at `product_family`/`product_model` grain and must **not** be used to infer an exact `product_variant`. Separately, NetApp's own KB documents a **label-printing defect** on this exact part number family — "X477A-R6 disk drive mislabeled as part number: X4477A-R6 on physical disk label" — a reminder that physical labels themselves are not always trustworthy ground truth (typo/mislabel risk exists even at the OEM's own factory).

**Access posture (2026).** NetApp's authoritative reference — **Hardware Universe (`hwu.netapp.com`)** — **requires a customer or partner account**; a third-party training-site walkthrough states this explicitly: "Hardware Universe differs from the documentation site in that it is not publicly accessible... you need to have a user account, which is a customer or a partner-level account." NetApp does maintain public per-topic KB articles (`kb.netapp.com`) that resolve specific narrow questions (e.g., the SFP+/Ethernet-cable NetApp-to-vendor cross-reference article, or the mislabel notice above) but nothing bulk or drive-specific in the same way. As with Dell, there is **no public bulk OEM→MPN source** for NetApp; what exists is scattered, narrow KB articles plus reseller pages.

## Recommended population strategy

Given that **three of the four vendor families (Dell, Lenovo/IBM, NetApp) have no public bulk cross-reference at all**, and the one that does (HPE PartSurfer) is a single-part web lookup with no sanctioned API, a **pre-seeded bulk import is not available** as a starting option for this alias table — unlike the ADR-0018 manufacturer spec catalog itself, which *can* bulk-ingest first-party datasheets. The practical options, in the order this research supports:

1. **Primary: lazily learn the alias table from dual-labeled listings already being ingested.** The evidence above (EMC SPS Pros, ServerSupply, Alta Technologies product pages; eBay/ServerPartDeals listing titles) shows that sellers in exactly this project's target market **routinely print both the OEM part number and the underlying manufacturer model number on the same page**, because their buyers search by both. This means the listing parser the entity-resolution report already recommends (hybrid rules + normalization, per [`entity-resolution-…`](entity-resolution-for-cross-marketplace-hard-drive-and-ssd-price-tracking.md)) can be extended with **OEM-PN-shaped token patterns per vendor family** — Dell DP/N (`\b0?[0-9A-Z]{5}\b` context-gated near "Dell"/"DP/N"), EMC TLA (`00[45]\d{6}`), HPE option/spare (`\d{6}-(?:B21|001|021|…)`), NetApp X-article (`X\d{2,4}[A-Z]?(?:-R\d)?`) — and whenever a listing's title/body contains **both** an OEM-shaped token and a manufacturer-MPN-shaped token, emit a `product_alias(alias_type=oem_part_number, …)` row linking the OEM token to the resolved `product_model`, with provenance recording it as **listing-derived, not catalog-authoritative** (a confidence/`source_kind` distinction the alias table should carry regardless of this recommendation, since ADR-0010 already separates catalog aliases from evidence). Over time, popular OEM part numbers accumulate corroborating listings from independent sellers, which raises confidence without any manual work.
2. **Secondary: a small manual backfill queue for high-value gaps**, exactly as ADR-0018 already names. When a listing's OEM PN has **no** co-occurring manufacturer MPN anywhere in the observed listing corpus, and the listing scores highly enough to matter (a recurring, high-volume, or high-score family), queue it for a **one-off, human-paced manual lookup** — HPE PartSurfer for HPE parts (fine at this volume and cadence), a targeted web/forum search for Dell/NetApp/Lenovo parts (ServeTheHome-style buyer's-guide posts, reseller product pages, r/homelab/r/DataHoarder threads all showed up as usable spot-check sources in this research). This keeps the one genuinely gated/manual-only step small and bounded, rather than trying to build automation against sites that don't offer one (NetApp Hardware Universe, Dell's reseller catalog) or that explicitly warn against scraping fragility (HPE PartSurfer, per its own community).
3. **Not recommended as primary: automating against HPE PartSurfer or third-party wrappers.** It's the only public source among the four, but it is single-part, has no official API, and the existing unofficial scrapers describe themselves as fragile. Reserve it for the manual backfill queue (option 2), not a scheduled ingest job — consistent with ADR-0018's cadence separation (the manufacturer catalog itself refreshes on a slow, deliberate cadence; an ad hoc PartSurfer lookup is rarer still and should not be scheduled at all).
4. **Explicitly do not attempt to resolve OEM PNs to exact `product_variant`.** The NetApp `X477A-R6` → {HGST `HUS724040ALS640`, WD `WD4001FYYG`} evidence and HPE's own "one assembly part number, multiple spare/option numbers" statement both show the OEM layer is coarser than the variant grain the canonical model already reserves for condition/packaging/warranty-channel (ADR-0010 rule 2). An OEM-PN-sourced alias should resolve matching to `product_family`/`product_model` and let `drive_spec` attributes (interface, capacity, RPM/DWPD) plus listing-parsed evidence narrow the variant, rather than trusting the OEM PN to imply one.

## Firmware suffixes and OEM-custom model strings in reported drive identity

- **Suffix conventions differ per OEM and generally encode qualification/build revision, not functional variants a buyer would care about**: HPE's assembly-number suffix (`-0A1` vs `-1A1` on otherwise-identical option numbers) was shown by an HPE community thread to be a build/rank difference invisible to the option and spare numbers. NetApp's `-R5`/`-R6` suffix on X-article numbers is a **revision code** on the same base part (`X477A-R5` and `X477A-R6` coexist as cross-referenced aliases in reseller listings), consistent with a running-change/requalification pattern rather than a capacity or interface change. Dell/EMC's TLA scheme bakes **capacity and interface/speed directly into the number** (a rare case where the OEM code is *more* semantically decodable than the manufacturer MPN alone), but the trailing serial-like digits still churn across qualification batches for the same nominal product.
- **Manufacturer model strings usually do survive into SMART/NVMe-identify output, even for OEM-sourced drives** — direct examples pulled from public forum `smartctl`/`nvme` output show manufacturer-native model strings (`Micron_7450_MTFDKCC15T3TFR`, `WDS100T1X0E-00AFY0`, `Samsung SSD 990 PRO 1TB`) reported as-is by consumer/OEM-sourced NVMe drives. This is the good case for entity resolution: a listing that quotes a `smartctl`/SMART-derived model string is quoting the manufacturer's own identity string, matchable directly against the ADR-0018 spec catalog.
- **The risk is concentrated in deep-OEM-qualified enterprise SAS/FC drives, not commodity SATA/NVMe.** A Dell PowerEdge community thread shows a RAID controller reporting **two drives with vendor ID `DELL`** and a third with **vendor ID `Seagate`** for what the poster expected to be identical hardware — i.e., OEM-custom firmware can overwrite the SCSI INQUIRY vendor/product fields entirely with the OEM's own branding on some enterprise SAS drives, while a differently-sourced unit of the "same" drive reports the manufacturer's native identity. Seagate support confirms this is intentional and one-directional: "Seagate can't help you because they don't have public tools to flash their drives with firmware other than their own (they do this in their factories for OEM's)" — meaning an OEM-branded enterprise drive's reported identity string is not just cosmetically different but is **produced by different firmware** than the retail-channel version of the same physical drive family, and OEM support tooling from Dell/HPE/etc. explicitly refuses to operate on retail-firmware drives and vice versa (further confirming these are treated as functionally distinct SKUs downstream, not just relabeled ones).
- **Practical implication for the listing parser**: title/description text is the more reliable place to find the manufacturer MPN for enterprise server-pull drives (sellers print it because buyers search for it, per the reseller-page evidence above), while a scraped SMART/NVMe-identify string quoted in a listing should be treated as **strong when it matches a manufacturer-shaped pattern**, but **not assumed present** — some fraction of genuinely OEM-firmware SAS/FC drives will only ever expose the OEM's own vendor/product strings at the protocol level, with the manufacturer MPN recoverable only from the physical label or the seller's own cross-reference text.

## Confidence and caveats

- **Confidence: medium.** The vendor-semantics claims (option/spare/assembly split, DP/N vs TLA, FRU/MultiFRU, X-article) are corroborated by multiple independent sources per vendor, including at least one official vendor document each. The **N:N verdict is well-evidenced for NetApp and HPE** (direct vendor statements plus a concrete dual-sourced-part example) and **reasonably evidenced for Dell/EMC** (firmware-release-note and TLA-churn evidence), but weaker for Lenovo/IBM, where the clearest documented fan-out (MultiFRU, leaded/unleaded) is **regional/compliance-driven** rather than confirmed manufacturer-substitution — treat the Lenovo/IBM 1:N claim as "designed-in regional/build fan-out, manufacturer-substitution unconfirmed but plausible by analogy."
- **Access-posture facts are time-sensitive (2026).** Dell/NetApp/Lenovo's lack of a public bulk source and HPE PartSurfer's lack of an official API were all confirmed as of this research date; any of these vendors could add or remove a public tool.
